YUDDHA¦ (WAR). (i) In ancient times in India war
<div n="lb"/>was considered a “Rājadharma”. A war declared
<div n="lb"/>under this law was known as “Dharmayuddha”
<div n="NI"/>(ii) It is forbidden to use a weapon describing it falsely
<div n="lb"/>as another weapon. The use of arrows heated in fire, is
<div n="lb"/>also against Dharmayuddha.
<div n="NI"/>(iii) It is also against the rules of Dharmayuddha to
<div n="lb"/>kill a person who gets down from the chariot, a eunuch,
<div n="lb"/>one with joined palms, one who squats on the ground,
<div n="lb"/>one who seeks refuge, one who is asleep, one who is
<div n="lb"/>naked, an unarmed person, one who has come to wit-
<div n="lb"/>ness the fight, one who is fighting with another, one
<div n="lb"/>whose weapon is broken, one who is bereaved by the
<div n="lb"/>death of a son or other relative, one who is vanquished,
<div n="lb"/>one who flees from battle, and one who refuses to attack
<div n="lb"/>in return etc.
<div n="NI"/>(iv) If a warrior fleeing from battlefield is killed by his
<div n="lb"/>enemy, he carries with him his master's sins.
<div n="NI"/>(v) All the grace earned by the young man who flees
<div n="lb"/>from battle, passes to his master.
<div n="NI"/>(vi) The soldier himself may take all booty in the
<div n="lb"/>battle except chariots, horses, elephants, umbrellas,
<div n="lb"/>wealth, corn, cows, women, weapons, silver and gold.
<div n="NI"/>(vii) All costly articles, seized in battle, such as gold,
<div n="lb"/>silver, jewels etc. are to be handed over to the King,
<div n="lb"/>according to the Vedas. (Manusmṛti, Chapter 7).
