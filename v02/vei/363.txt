{@Upa-niṣad@}¦ in the Brāhmaṇas<sup>1</sup> normally denotes the ‘secret
<div n="lb"/>sense’ of some word or text, sometimes the ‘secret rule’ of the
<div n="lb"/>mendicant. But in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad<sup>2</sup> it is already
<div n="lb"/>used in the plural as the designation of a class of writings, no
<div n="lb"/>doubt actually existing and similar to the Upaniṣads in the
<div n="lb"/>nature of their subject-matter and its treatment. Similarly
<div n="lb"/>the sections of the Taittirīya Upaniṣad end with the words
<div n="lb"/>{%ity upaniṣad.%} The Aitareya Āraṇyaka<sup>3</sup> commences its third
<div n="lb"/>part with the title ‘The Upaniṣad of the Saṃhitā,’ and the
<div n="lb"/>title occurs also in the Śāṅkhāyana Āraṇyaka.<sup>4</sup> The exact
<div n="lb"/>primary sense of the expression is doubtful. The natural
<div n="lb"/>derivation, adopted by Max Müller<sup>5</sup> and usual ever since,
<div n="lb"/>makes the word mean firstly a session of pupils, hence secret
<div n="lb"/>doctrine, and secondly the title of a work on secret doctrine.
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg,<sup>6</sup> however, traces the use of the word to the earlier
[Page1-092+ 42]
<div n="lb"/>sense of ‘worship’ ({%cf. upāsana%}). Deussen<sup>7</sup> considers the
<div n="lb"/>original sense to have been ‘secret word,’ next ‘secret text,’
<div n="lb"/>and then ‘secret import,’ but this order of meaning is im-
<div n="lb"/>probable. Hopkins<sup>8</sup> suggests that Upaniṣad denotes a sub-
<div n="lb"/>sidiary treatise, but this sense does not account naturally for
<div n="lb"/>the common use as ‘secret meaning,’ which is far more
<div n="lb"/>frequent than any other.
