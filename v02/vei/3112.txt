{@Vraja@}¦ denotes in the first instance, in the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> the place
<div n="lb"/>to which the cattle resort (from {%vraj,%} ‘go’), the ‘feeding ground’
<div n="lb"/>to which the milk-giving animals go out<sup>2</sup> in the morning from
<div n="lb"/>the village ({@Grāma@}), while the others stay in it all day and
<div n="lb"/>night.<sup>3</sup> Secondarily it denotes the ‘herd’<sup>4</sup> itself. This is
<div n="lb"/>Geldner's view,<sup>5</sup> which seems clearly better than that of Roth<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>who regards Vraja as primarily the ‘enclosure’ (from {%vṛj%}), and
<div n="lb"/>only thence the ‘herd’; for the Vraja does not normally mean
<div n="lb"/>an ‘enclosure’ at all: the Vedic cattle were not stall-fed as a
<div n="lb"/>general rule. In some passages, however, ‘pen,’<sup>7</sup> in others
<div n="lb"/>‘stall,’<sup>8</sup> is certainly meant. The word is often used in the
<div n="lb"/>myth of the robbing of the kine.<sup>9</sup> It occasionally denotes a
<div n="lb"/>‘cistern.’<sup>10</sup>
