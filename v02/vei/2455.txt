2. {@Māna@}¦ is the name of a man occurring in several passages
<div n="lb"/>of the Rigveda. In one place<sup>1</sup> express mention is made of his
<div n="lb"/>son ({%sūnu%}), by whom, despite Bergaigne's view to the contrary,<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>{@Agastya@} must be meant. In another passage,<sup>3</sup> apparently the
<div n="lb"/>same meaning applies to Māna — that is, Agastya as ‘a Māna.’
<div n="lb"/>In a third passage<sup>4</sup> the expression {%sūnave Mānena%} has been
<div n="lb"/>held by Sieg<sup>5</sup> to be an inversion of {%Mānasya sūnunā,%} ‘by the
<div n="lb"/>son of Māna’ — {%i.e.,%} Agastya; but it seems more likely<sup>6</sup> that
<div n="lb"/>either {%sūnor Māna%} is the fuller form of Agastya's name (‘pride
[Page2-153+ 39]
<div n="lb"/>of the son,’ with reference to his high ancestry), or that the
<div n="lb"/>son<sup>7</sup> of Māna (= Agastya) is alluded to as interested in
<div n="lb"/>{@Viśpalā.@}
<div n="P"/>The Mānas — that is, the descendants of Māna, are in several
<div n="lb"/>passages alluded to as singers.<sup>8</sup> {%Cf.%} {@Mānya, Māndārya.@}
