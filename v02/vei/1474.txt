{@Nakṣatra@}¦ is a word of obscure origin and derivation. The
<div n="lb"/>Indian interpreters already show a great divergence of opinion
<div n="lb"/>as to its primary meaning. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>1</sup> re-
<div n="lb"/>solves it into {%na-kṣatra%} (‘no power’), explaining it by a legend.
<div n="lb"/>The Nirukta<sup>2</sup> refers it to the root {%nakṣ,%} ‘obtain,’ following the
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa.<sup>3</sup> Aufrecht<sup>4</sup> and Weber<sup>5</sup> derived it from
<div n="lb"/>{%nakta-tra,%} ‘guardian of night,’ and more recently<sup>6</sup> the derivation
<div n="lb"/>from {%nak-kṣatra,%} ‘having rule over night,’ seems to be gaining
<div n="lb"/>acceptance. The generic meaning of the word therefore seems
<div n="lb"/>to be ‘star.’
<div n="P"/>{%The Nakṣatras as Stars in the Rigveda and Later.%} — The sense
<div n="lb"/>of ‘star’ appears to be adequate for all or nearly all the passages
<div n="lb"/>in which Nakṣatra occurs in the Rigveda.<sup>7</sup> The same sense
<div n="lb"/>occurs in the later Saṃhitās also: the sun and the Nakṣatras are
<div n="lb"/>mentioned together,<sup>8</sup> or the sun, the moon, and the Nakṣatras,<sup>9</sup>
<div n="lb"/>or the moon and the Nakṣatras,<sup>10</sup> or the Nakṣatras alone;<sup>11</sup>
[Page1-410+ 55]
<div n="lb"/>but there is no necessity to attribute to the word the sense of
<div n="lb"/>‘lunar mansion’ in these passages.
<div n="P"/>On the other hand, the names of at least three of the
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras in the later sense occur in the Rigveda. Tiṣya,<sup>12</sup>
<div n="lb"/>however, does not seem to be mentioned as a lunar mansion.
<div n="lb"/>With {@Aghās@} (plur.) and {@Arjunī@} (dual)<sup>13</sup> the case is different:
<div n="lb"/>it seems probable that they are the later lunar mansions called
<div n="lb"/>Maghās (plur.) and Phalgunī (dual). The names appear to
<div n="lb"/>have been deliberately changed in the Rigveda, and it must be
<div n="lb"/>remembered that the hymn in which they occur, the wedding
<div n="lb"/>hymn of Sūryā, has no claim to great age.<sup>14</sup> Ludwig<sup>15</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>Zimmer<sup>16</sup> have seen other references to the Nakṣatras as
<div n="lb"/>27 in the Rigveda,<sup>17</sup> but these seem most improbable. Nor
<div n="lb"/>do the adjectives {%revatī%} (‘rich’) and {%punarvasā%} (‘bringing
<div n="lb"/>wealth again’) in another hymn<sup>18</sup> appear to refer to the
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras.
<div n="P"/>{%The Nakṣatras as Lunar Mansions.%} — In several passages of the
<div n="lb"/>later Saṃhitās the connexion of the moon and the Nakṣatras
<div n="lb"/>is conceived of as a marriage union. Thus in the Kāṭhaka<sup>19</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and Taittirīya Saṃhitās<sup>20</sup> it is expressly stated that Soma was
<div n="lb"/>wedded to the mansions, but dwelt only with Rohiṇī; the
<div n="lb"/>others being angry, he had ultimately to undertake to live with
<div n="lb"/>them all equally. Weber<sup>21</sup> hence deduced that the Nakṣatras
<div n="lb"/>were regarded as of equal extent, but this is to press the texts
<div n="lb"/>unduly, except in the sense of approximate equality. The
<div n="lb"/>number of the mansions is not stated as 27 in the story told in
<div n="lb"/>the two Saṃhitās: the Taittīriya has 33, and the Kāṭhaka no
[Page1-411+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>number; but 27 appears as their number in the list which is
<div n="lb"/>found in the Taittirīya Saṃhitā<sup>22</sup> and elsewhere.<sup>23</sup> The
<div n="lb"/>number 28 is much less well attested: in one passage of the
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>24</sup> Abhijit is practically marked as a new
<div n="lb"/>comer, though in a later book,<sup>25</sup> in the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā,<sup>26</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and in the Atharvaveda list,<sup>27</sup> it has found acceptance. It is
<div n="lb"/>perfectly possible that 28 is the earlier number, and that Abhijit
<div n="lb"/>dropped out because it was faint, or too far north, or because
<div n="lb"/>27 was a more mystic (3×3×3) number: it is significant that
<div n="lb"/>the Chinese Sieou and the Arabic Manāzil are 28 in number.<sup>28</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Weber,<sup>29</sup> however, believes that 27 is the older number in
<div n="lb"/>India.
<div n="P"/>The meaning of the number is easily explained when it is
<div n="lb"/>remembered that a periodic month occupies something between
<div n="lb"/>27 and 28 days, more nearly the former number. Such a month
<div n="lb"/>is in fact recognized in the Lāṭyāyana<sup>30</sup> and Nidāna Sūtras<sup>31</sup>
<div n="lb"/>as consisting of 27 days, 12 months making a year of 324 days,
<div n="lb"/>a Nakṣatra year, or with an intercalary month, a year of
<div n="lb"/>351 days. The Nidāna Sūtra<sup>32</sup> makes an attempt to introduce
<div n="lb"/>the Nakṣatra reckoning into the civil or solar ({%sāvana%}) year
<div n="lb"/>of 360 days, for it holds that the sun spends 13(1/3) days in
<div n="lb"/>each Nakṣatra (13(1/3)×27 = 360). But the month of 27 or
<div n="lb"/>28 days plays no part in the chronological calculations of the
<div n="lb"/>Veda.<sup>33</sup>
<div n="P"/>{%The Names of the Nakṣatras.%} — In addition to the two men-
<div n="lb"/>tioned in the Rigveda, the earlier Atharvaveda<sup>34</sup> gives the
[Page1-412+ 61]
<div n="lb"/>names of Jyeṣṭhaghnī<sup>35</sup> (the later Jyeṣṭhā) and Vicṛtau,<sup>36</sup> which
<div n="lb"/>are mentioned as in close connexion, and of Revatīs (plural)
<div n="lb"/>and Kṛttikās.<sup>37</sup> With reference to possible times for the
<div n="lb"/>ceremony of the Agnyādhāna, or ‘laying of the sacred fires,’
<div n="lb"/>the Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā,<sup>38</sup> the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā,<sup>39</sup> and the
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>40</sup> mention the Nakṣatras called Kṛttikās,
<div n="lb"/>Rohiṇī, Phalgunyas, Hasta; the latter Brāhmaṇa adds Punar-
<div n="lb"/>vasū, and in an additional remark<sup>41</sup> excludes Pūrve Phalgunī
<div n="lb"/>in favour of Uttare Phalgunī. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>42</sup>
<div n="lb"/>adds Mṛgaśīrṣa and Citrā as possibilities. On the other hand,
<div n="lb"/>Punarvasū is recommended by all authorities<sup>43</sup> as suitable for
<div n="lb"/>the Punarādheya, ‘relaying of the sacred fires,’ which takes
<div n="lb"/>place if the first fire has failed to effect the aim of its existence,
<div n="lb"/>the prosperity of the sacrificer.<sup>44</sup> The Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā,<sup>45</sup>
<div n="lb"/>however, allows Anurādhās also.
<div n="P"/>In the ceremony of the Agnicayana, or ‘piling of the fire-
<div n="lb"/>altar,’ the bricks are assumed to be equal in number to the
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras. The bricks number 756, and they are equated to
<div n="lb"/>27 Nakṣatras multiplied by 27 secondary Nakṣatras, reckoned
<div n="lb"/>as 720 (instead of 729), with the addition of 36 days, the length
<div n="lb"/>of an intercalary month. Nothing can be usefully derived
<div n="lb"/>from this piece of priestly nonsense.<sup>46</sup> But in connexion with
<div n="lb"/>this ceremony the Yajurveda Saṃhitās<sup>47</sup> enumerate the 27
[Page1-413+ 50]
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras, and these lists<sup>48</sup> may be given {%in extenso%} as

<div n="P"/>The Taittirīya Brahmaṇa<sup>49</sup> has a list of the Nakṣatras
<div n="lb"/>which agrees generally with the list of the Saṃhitās. It runs
<div n="lb"/>as follows: Kṛttikās, Rohiṇī, Invakās, Bāhū (dual), Tiṣya,
<div n="lb"/>Āśleṣās, Maghās, Pūrve Phalgunī, Uttare Phalgunī, Hasta,
<div n="lb"/>Citrā, Niṣṭyā, Viśākhe, Anūrādhās, Rohiṇī, Mūlabarhaṇī,
<div n="lb"/>Pūrvā Aṣāḍhās, Uttarā Aṣāḍhās, Śroṇā, Śraviṣṭhās, Śatabhiṣaj,
<div n="lb"/>Pūrve Proṣṭhapadās, Uttare Proṣṭhapadās, Revatī, Aśvayujau,
<div n="lb"/>Apabharaṇīs. In a later book,<sup>50</sup> however, the list grows to 28,
[Page1-414+ 50]
<div n="lb"/>and the full moon is inserted after number 14, and the new
<div n="lb"/>moon after number 28, as an attempt to bring the Nākṣatra
<div n="lb"/>(lunar) month into accordance with the Sāvana (solar) month
<div n="lb"/>of 30 days. The names in this second list are as in the
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitās with the following exceptions. The seven stars of
<div n="lb"/>the Kṛttikās are named as Ambā, Dulā, Nitatnī, Abhrayantī,
<div n="lb"/>Meghayantī, Varṣayantī, Cupuṇīkā, names found also in the
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya<sup>51</sup> and Kāṭhaka Saṃhitās.<sup>52</sup> Beside Mṛgaśīrṣa, Invakās
<div n="lb"/>are also mentioned.<sup>53</sup> Then come Ārdrā, Punarvasū, Tiṣya,
<div n="lb"/>Āśreṣās, Maghās (beside which Anaghās, Agadās, and Arun-
<div n="lb"/>dhatīs are also mentioned), Phalgunyas (but elsewhere in the
<div n="lb"/>dual, Phalgunyau),<sup>54</sup> Phalgunyas, Hasta, Citrā, Niṣṭyā, Viśākhe,
<div n="lb"/>Anūrādhās, Jyeṣṭhā, Mūla, Aṣāḍhās, Aṣāḍhās, Abhijit, Śronā,
<div n="lb"/>Śraviṣṭhās, Śatabhiṣaj, Proṣṭhapadās, Proṣṭhapadās, Revatī,
<div n="lb"/>Aśvayujau, Bharaṇyas, but also Apabharaṇīs.<sup>55</sup> Abhijit, which
<div n="lb"/>occurs also in an earlier part of the Brāhmaṇa,<sup>56</sup> is perhaps
<div n="lb"/>interpolated. But Weber's<sup>57</sup> argument that Abhijit is out of
<div n="lb"/>place in this list because Brāhmaṇa is here mentioned as the
<div n="lb"/>28th Nakṣatra, loses some force from the fact (of course unknown
<div n="lb"/>to him) that the list in the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>58</sup> contains
<div n="lb"/>28 Nakṣatras, including Abhijit, and adds Brāhmaṇa at the end
<div n="lb"/>as another.
<div n="P"/>In another passage<sup>59</sup> the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa divides the
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras into two sets, the Deva Nakṣatras and the Yama
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras, being 1-14 and 15-27 (with the omission of Abhijit)
<div n="lb"/>respectively. This division corresponds with one in the third
<div n="lb"/>book of the Brāhmaṇa<sup>60</sup> where the days of the light half of
<div n="lb"/>the month and those of the dark half are equated with the
<div n="lb"/>Nakṣatras. The Brāhmaṇa treats the former series as south,
<div n="lb"/>the latter as north; but this has no relation to facts, and
<div n="lb"/>can only be regarded as a ritual absurdity.
<div n="P"/>The late nineteenth book of the Atharvaveda contains a
<div n="lb"/>list<sup>61</sup> of the Nakṣatras, including Abhijit. The names here
[Page1-415+ 53]
<div n="lb"/>given are: Kṛttikās, Rohiṇī, Mṛgaśiras, Ārdrā, Punarvasū,
<div n="lb"/>Puṣya, Āśleṣās, Maghās, Pūrvā Phalgunyau ({%sic%}),<sup>62</sup> Hasta,
<div n="lb"/>Citrā, Svāti (masc.),<sup>63</sup> Viśākhe, Anurādhā,<sup>64</sup> Jyeṣṭhā, Mūla,
<div n="lb"/>Pūrvā Aṣāḍhās,<sup>65</sup> Uttarā Aṣāḍhās, Abhijit, Śravaṇa, Śraviṣṭhās,
<div n="lb"/>Śatabhiṣaj, Dvayā Proṣṭhapadā, Revatī, Aśvayujau, Bharaṇyas.
<div n="P"/>{%The Position of the Nakṣatras.%} — There is nothing definite in
<div n="lb"/>Vedic literature regarding the position of most of the Nakṣatras,
<div n="lb"/>but the later astronomy precisely locates all of them, and its
<div n="lb"/>statements agree on the whole satisfactorily with what is said
<div n="lb"/>in the earlier texts, though Weber<sup>66</sup> was inclined to doubt this.
