{@Dant, Danta,@}¦ ‘tooth,’ is frequently mentioned from the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda onwards.<sup>1</sup> Cleansing ({%dhāv%}) the teeth was an ordinary
<div n="lb"/>act, especially in preparation for a sacrifice, and accompanied
<div n="lb"/>bathing, shaving of the hair and beard ({%keśa-śmaśru%}), and the
<div n="lb"/>cutting of the nails.<sup>2</sup> A hymn of the Atharvaveda<sup>3</sup> celebrates
<div n="lb"/>the appearance of the first two teeth of a child, though its
<div n="lb"/>exact interpretation is doubtful.<sup>4</sup> In the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>there is a reference to a child's first teeth falling out. The
<div n="lb"/>word seems in the Rigveda<sup>6</sup> once to denote an elephant's tusk.
<div n="lb"/>Whether dentistry was practised is doubtful. The occurrence
<div n="lb"/>in the Aitareya Āraṇyaka<sup>7</sup> of {@Hiraṇya-dant,@} ‘gold-toothed,’ as
<div n="lb"/>the name of a man, is perhaps significant, especially as it is
<div n="lb"/>certain that the stopping of teeth with gold was known at
<div n="lb"/>Rome as early as the legislation of the Twelve Tables.<sup>8</sup>
