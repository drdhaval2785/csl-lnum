{@Ṛṣi,@}¦ ‘seer,’ is primarily a composer of hymns to the gods.
<div n="lb"/>In the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> reference is often made to previous singers
<div n="lb"/>and to contemporary poets. Old poems were inherited and
<div n="lb"/>refurbished by members of the composer's family,<sup>2</sup> but the
<div n="lb"/>great aim of the singers was to produce new and approved
<div n="lb"/>hymns.<sup>3</sup> It is not till the time of the Brāhmaṇas that the
[Page1-116+ 62]
<div n="lb"/>composition of hymns appears to have fallen into disuse,<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>though poetry was still produced, for example, in the form of
<div n="lb"/>{@Gāthās,@} which the priests were required to compose them-
<div n="lb"/>selves<sup>5</sup> and sing to the accompaniment of the lute at the
<div n="lb"/>sacrifice. The Ṛṣi was the most exalted of Brāhmaṇas,<sup>6</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>his skill, which is often compared with that of a carpenter,<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>was regarded as heaven-sent.<sup>8</sup> The Purohita, whether as Hotṛ
<div n="lb"/>or as Brahman (see {@Ṛtvij@}), was a singer.<sup>9</sup> No doubt the Ṛṣis
<div n="lb"/>were normally<sup>10</sup> attached to the houses of the great, the petty
<div n="lb"/>kings of Vedic times, or the nobles of the royal household.
<div n="lb"/>Nor need it be doubted that occasionally<sup>11</sup> the princes them-
<div n="lb"/>selves essayed poetry: a Rājanyarṣi, the prototype of the later
<div n="lb"/>Rājarṣi or ‘royal seer,’ who appears in the Pañcaviṃśa
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa,<sup>12</sup> though he must be mythical as Oldenberg<sup>13</sup>
<div n="lb"/>points out, indicates that kings cultivated poetry<sup>14</sup> just as later
<div n="lb"/>they engaged in philosophic disputations.<sup>15</sup> Normally, how-
<div n="lb"/>ever, the poetical function is Brahminical, Viśāmitra and others
<div n="lb"/>not being kings, but merely Brāhmaṇas, in the Rigveda.
<div n="P"/>In the later literature the Ṛṣis are the poets of the hymns
<div n="lb"/>preserved in the Saṃhitās. a Ṛṣi being regularly<sup>16</sup> cited when
<div n="lb"/>a Vedic Saṃhitā is quoted. Moreover, the Ṛṣis become the
<div n="lb"/>representatives of a sacred past, and are regarded as holy sages,
[Page1-117+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>whose deeds are narrated as if they were the deeds of gods or
<div n="lb"/>Asuras.<sup>17</sup> They are typified by a particular group of seven,<sup>18</sup>
<div n="lb"/>mentioned four times in the Rigveda,<sup>19</sup> several times in the
<div n="lb"/>later Saṃhitās,<sup>20</sup> and enumerated in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka
<div n="lb"/>Upaniṣad<sup>21</sup> as Gotama, Bharadvāja, Viśvāmitra, Jamadagni,
<div n="lb"/>Vasiṣṭha, Kaśyapa, and Atri. In the Rigveda itself Kutsa,<sup>22</sup>
<div n="lb"/>Atri,<sup>23</sup> Rebha,<sup>24</sup> Agastya,<sup>25</sup> the Kuśikas,<sup>26</sup> Vasiṣṭha,<sup>27</sup> Vyaśva,<sup>28</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and others appear as Ṛṣis; and the Atharvaveda<sup>29</sup> contains a
<div n="lb"/>long list, including Aṅgiras, Agastī, Jamadagni, Atri, Kaśyapa,
<div n="lb"/>Vasiṣṭha, Bharadvāja, Gaviṣṭhira, Viśvāmitra, Kutsa, Kakṣīvant,
<div n="lb"/>Kaṇva, Medhātithi, Triśoka, Uśanā Kāvya, Gotama, and
<div n="lb"/>Mudgala.
<div n="P"/>Competition among the bards appears to have been known.
<div n="lb"/>This is one of the sides of the riddle poetry ({@Brahmodya@}) that
<div n="lb"/>forms a distinctive feature<sup>30</sup> of the Vedic ritual of the Aśva-
<div n="lb"/>medha, or horse sacrifice. In the Upaniṣad period such
<div n="lb"/>competitions were quite frequent. The most famous was that
<div n="lb"/>of Yājñavalkya, which was held at the court of Janaka of
<div n="lb"/>Videha, as detailed in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad,<sup>31</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>which was a source of annoyance to {@Ajātaśatru@} of {@Kāśī.@}<sup>32</sup>
<div n="lb"/>According to an analogous practice, a Brāhmaṇa, like {@Uddālaka@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Aruṇi,@} would go about disputing with all he came across, and
<div n="lb"/>compete with them for a prize of money.<sup>33</sup>
