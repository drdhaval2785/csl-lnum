{@Maṇḍūka@}¦ is the name of ‘frog’ in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and later,<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the feminine Maṇḍūkī also occurring.<sup>3</sup> The famous frog hymn
<div n="lb"/>of the Rigveda<sup>4</sup> compares with Brahmins the frogs croaking
[Page2-121+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>as they awake to activity at the beginning of the rains. It has
<div n="lb"/>been explained by Max Müller<sup>5</sup> as a satire on the Brahmins.
<div n="lb"/>Geldner,<sup>6</sup> agreeing with this view, thinks that it is directed by
<div n="lb"/>its {@Vasiṣṭha@} composer against rival Brahmins, probably the
<div n="lb"/>{@Viśvāmitras.@}<sup>7</sup> The view, however, which interprets the hymn
<div n="lb"/>as a rain charm<sup>8</sup> seems on the whole more likely. The frog,
<div n="lb"/>from its connexion with water, was considered to have cooling
<div n="lb"/>properties. Thus after the burning of the dead body the frog
<div n="lb"/>is invited to come to the spot where the cremation has taken
<div n="lb"/>place in order to cool it down.<sup>9</sup> Similarly the frog is invoked
<div n="lb"/>in the Atharvaveda against the fire of fever.<sup>10</sup>
