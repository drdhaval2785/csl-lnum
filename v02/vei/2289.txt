{@Bhṛgu@}¦ is a sage of almost entirely mythical character in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda and later. He counts as a son of Varuṇa,<sup>1</sup> bearing
<div n="lb"/>the patronymic Vāruṇi.<sup>2</sup> In the plural the Bhṛgus are
<div n="lb"/>repeatedly<sup>3</sup> alluded to as devoted to the fire cult. They are
<div n="lb"/>clearly<sup>4</sup> no more than a group of ancient priests and ancestors
<div n="lb"/>with an eponymous Bhṛgu<sup>5</sup> in the Rigveda, except in three
<div n="lb"/>passages,<sup>6</sup> where they are evidently regarded as an historic
<div n="lb"/>family. It is not clear, however, whether they were priests or
<div n="lb"/>warriors: in the battle of the ten kings the Bhṛgus appear with
<div n="lb"/>the {@Druhyus,@} perhaps as their priests, but this is not certain.<sup>7</sup>
<div n="P"/>In the later literature the Bhṛgus are a real family, with sub-
<div n="lb"/>divisions like the {@Aitaśāyana,@} according to the Kauṣītaki
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa.<sup>8</sup> The Bhṛgus are mentioned as priests in connexion
<div n="lb"/>with various rites, such as the Agnisthāpana<sup>9</sup> and the Daśa-
<div n="lb"/>peyakratu.<sup>10</sup> In many passages they are conjoined with the
<div n="lb"/>{@Aṅgirases.@}<sup>11</sup> the close association of the two families is shown
[Page2-110+ 43]
<div n="lb"/>by the fact that Cyavana is called either a Bhārgava or an
<div n="lb"/>Āṅgirasa in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa.<sup>12</sup> In the Atharvaveda<sup>13</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the name of Bhṛgu is selected to exemplify the dangers incurred
<div n="lb"/>by the oppressors of Brahmans: the {@Sṛñjaya Vaitahavyas@}
<div n="lb"/>perish in consequence of an attack on Bhṛgu. In the Aitareya
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>14</sup> also Bhṛgu has this representative character. {%Cf.%}
