2. {@Brahman@}¦ is found in many passages of the Rigveda and
<div n="lb"/>later in the sense of ‘priest.’ In many passages of the Rigveda<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>he is referred to as praising the gods; in others<sup>2</sup> the sense of
<div n="lb"/>‘priest’ is adequate. In not a few cases<sup>3</sup> the priesthood as a
<div n="lb"/>profession is clearly alluded to, nor is there any reason to doubt<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>that in all cases the word has the technical sense of a member
<div n="lb"/>of the priesthood. There is, however, considerable doubt as to
<div n="lb"/>the number of cases in the Rigveda, where it has the technical
[Page2-078+ 60]
<div n="lb"/>sense of the priest who guides the sacrifice generally. It is
<div n="lb"/>undoubtedly found in that sense, both Muir<sup>5</sup> and Roth<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>recognizing instances of its being used thus. Geldner,<sup>7</sup> how-
<div n="lb"/>ever, is anxious to find that sense in a large number of passages,
<div n="lb"/>and insists that the {@Purohita@} was normally a Brahman in the
<div n="lb"/>narrower sense. Oldenberg,<sup>8</sup> on the other hand, holds with
<div n="lb"/>greater probability that in most of the passages adduced
<div n="lb"/>Brahman means simply ‘priest,’ and that the Purohita, who
<div n="lb"/>was essentially not a member of the ordinary body of sacri-
<div n="lb"/>ficing priests ({@Ṛtvij@}), was, when he officiated at the sacrifice,
<div n="lb"/>more usually the Hotṛ priest, and only later became the
<div n="lb"/>Brahman. This change he regards as having taken place when
<div n="lb"/>the importance of the hymns declined, and most weight was
<div n="lb"/>laid on the functions of the priest who superintended the sacrifice
<div n="lb"/>as a whole, and by his magic repaired the flaws in the sacrifice.<sup>9</sup>
<div n="lb"/>In the later literature both senses of the word are quite
<div n="lb"/>common.<sup>10</sup>
