{@Mudgala@}¦ and {@Mudgalanī,@} ‘Mudgala's wife,’ both figure in
<div n="lb"/>a hopelessly obscure hymn of the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> variously inter-
<div n="lb"/>preted by Pischel<sup>2</sup> and Geldner<sup>3</sup> and von Bradke<sup>4</sup> as telling
<div n="lb"/>of a real chariot race in which, despite difficulties, Mudgala
<div n="lb"/>won by his wife's aid. The Indian tradition is as variant as
<div n="lb"/>the interpretations of modern authorities. Ṣaḍguruśiṣya<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>explains that Mudgala's oxen were stolen, that he pursued the
<div n="lb"/>thieves with the one old ox he had left, and that hurling his
<div n="lb"/>hammer ({%dru-ghaṇa%}) he caught the marauders. Yāska,<sup>6</sup> on the
<div n="lb"/>other hand, says that Mudgala won a race with a {%drughaṇa%}
<div n="lb"/>and an ox instead of with two oxen. It is pretty clear that,
<div n="lb"/>as Roth<sup>7</sup> observed, the tradition is merely a guess, and a bad
<div n="lb"/>one, at the meaning of an obscure hymn, and this view is
[Page2-167+ 55]
<div n="lb"/>accepted by Oldenberg.<sup>8</sup> Bloomfield<sup>9</sup> has interpreted the
<div n="lb"/>legend as one of heavenly, not of human, events. Mudgala,
<div n="lb"/>probably a variant form of Mudgara,<sup>10</sup> which in the later
<div n="lb"/>language means a hammer or a similar weapon, may be meant
<div n="lb"/>as a personification of the thunderbolt of Indra, rather than
<div n="lb"/>a real man.<sup>11</sup> Later<sup>12</sup> Mudgala is a mythical sage.
