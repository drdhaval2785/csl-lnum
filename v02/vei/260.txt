{@Ārjīka@}¦<sup>1</sup> and {@Ārjīkīya@}<sup>2</sup> (masc.), {@Ārjīkīyā@}<sup>3</sup> (fem.). — The two
<div n="lb"/>masculine forms probably denote the people or land, while the
<div n="lb"/>feminine word designates the river of the land. Hillebrandt<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>locates the country in or near Kaśmir, as Arrian<sup>5</sup> mentions
<div n="lb"/>Arsaces, brother of Abhisares, who presumably took his name
<div n="lb"/>from his people, and Abhisāra bordered on Kaśmir. Pischel<sup>6</sup>
<div n="lb"/>accepts Ārjīka as designating a country, which he, however,
<div n="lb"/>thinks cannot be identified. But neither Roth<sup>7</sup> nor Zimmer<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>recognizes the word as a proper name. On the other hand,
<div n="lb"/>all authorities agree in regarding Ārjīkīyā as the name of a
[Page1-063+ 38]
<div n="lb"/>river. {@Roth@}<sup>9</sup> does so in one passage<sup>10</sup> only, elsewhere seeing
<div n="lb"/>references to Soma vessels; but it seems necessary to treat the
<div n="lb"/>word alike in all passages containing it. Zimmer does not
<div n="lb"/>locate the river, and Pischel denies the possibility of its
<div n="lb"/>identification. Hillebrandt thinks it may have been the
<div n="lb"/>Upper Indus, or the {@Vitastā@} (the Jhelum), or some other
<div n="lb"/>stream. Grassmann follows Yāska<sup>11</sup> in identifying it with the
<div n="lb"/>the {@Vipāś@} (Beäs), but this is rendered improbable by the
<div n="lb"/>position of the name in the hymn in praise of rivers ({%nadī-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%stuti%}).<sup>12</sup> Brunnhofer<sup>13</sup> identifies it with the Arghesan, a tributary
<div n="lb"/>of the Arghanab.
