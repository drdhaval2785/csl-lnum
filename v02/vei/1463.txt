{@Dhruva@}¦ in the Sūtras<sup>1</sup> denotes the pole star, being mentioned
<div n="lb"/>in connexion with the marriage ritual, in which the star is
<div n="lb"/>pointed out to the bride as an emblem of constancy. In the

<div n="lb"/>Maitrāyaṇī Upaniṣad,<sup>2</sup> a late work, the movement of the
<div n="lb"/>Dhruva ({%dhruvasya pracalanam%}) is mentioned, but this can hardly
<div n="lb"/>be interpreted as referring to an actual observed motion of the
<div n="lb"/>nominal pole star,<sup>3</sup> but rather to an extraordinary event, such
<div n="lb"/>as a destruction of the world, as Cowell understood the
<div n="lb"/>expression.<sup>4</sup> Jacobi<sup>5</sup> sees in the motion of the Dhruva the
<div n="lb"/>possibility of fixing a date, on the ground that the only star
<div n="lb"/>which could have been deemed a pole star, as ‘immovable,’ was
<div n="lb"/>one ({%a%} Draconis) of the third millennium B.C. But this attempt
<div n="lb"/>to extract chronology from the name of the star is of very
<div n="lb"/>doubtful validity.<sup>6</sup>
