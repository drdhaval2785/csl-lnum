{@Ni-ṣāda@}¦ is found in the later Saṃhitās and the Brāhmaṇas.<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>The word seems to denote not so much a particular tribe, but
<div n="lb"/>to be the general term for the non-Āryan tribes who were not
<div n="lb"/>under Āryan control, as the Śūdras were, for Aupamanyava<sup>2</sup>
<div n="lb"/>took the five peoples ({%pañca janāḥ%}) to be the four castes ({%catvāro%}
[Page1-454+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>{%varṇāḥ%}) and the Niṣādas, and the commentator Mahīdhara
<div n="lb"/>explains the word where it occurs in the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>3</sup>
<div n="lb"/>as meaning a Bhilla, or Bhīl. A village of Niṣādas is men-
<div n="lb"/>tioned in the Lāṭyāyana Śrauta Sūtra,<sup>4</sup> and a Niṣāda {@Sthapati,@}
<div n="lb"/>a leader of some kind, is referred to in the Kātyāyana Śrauta
<div n="lb"/>Sūtra<sup>5</sup> and in a Brāhmaṇa cited by the scholiast on that
<div n="lb"/>passage. Weber<sup>6</sup> thinks that the Niṣādas were the settled
<div n="lb"/>aborigines (form {%ni,%} ‘down,’ and {%sad,%} ‘settle’), a view sup-
<div n="lb"/>ported by the fact that the ritual of the Viśvajit sacrifice<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>requires a temporary residence with Niṣādas; for the Niṣādas
<div n="lb"/>who would permit an Āryan to reside temporarily amongst
<div n="lb"/>them must have been partially amenable to Āryan influence.
<div n="lb"/>But the name might easily be applied to the whole body of
<div n="lb"/>aborigines outside the Āryan organization. Von Schroeder<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>thinks that the Niṣādas were most probably identical with the
<div n="lb"/>Nysæans, who, according to the Greek account, sent an
<div n="lb"/>embassy to Alexander when he was in the territory of the
<div n="lb"/>Aśvakas, but this identification is doubtful.
