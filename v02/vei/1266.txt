{@Daśan,@}¦ ‘ten,’ forms the basis of the numerical system of the
<div n="lb"/>Vedic Indians, as it does of the Āryan people generally. But
<div n="lb"/>it is characteristic of India<sup>1</sup> that there should be found at a
<div n="lb"/>very early period long series of names for very high numerals,
<div n="lb"/>whereas the Āryan knowledge did not go beyond 1,000. In
<div n="lb"/>the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>2</sup> the list is 1; 10; 100; 1,000; 10,000
<div n="lb"/>({%ayuta%}); 100,000 ({%niyuta%}); 1,000,000 ({%prayuta%}); 10,000,000 ({%arbuda%});
<div n="lb"/>100,000,000 ({%nyarbuda%}); 1,000,000,000 ({%samudra%}); 10,000,000,000
<div n="lb"/>({%madhya%}); 100,000,000,000 ({%anta%}); 1,000,000,000,000 ({%parārdha%}).
<div n="lb"/>In the Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā<sup>3</sup> the list is the same, but {%niyuta%} and
<div n="lb"/>{%prayuta%} exchange places, and after {%nyarbuda%} a new figure
<div n="lb"/>({%badva%}) intervenes, thus increasing {%samudra%} to 10,000,000,000,
<div n="lb"/>and so on. The Taittirīya Saṃhitā has in two places<sup>4</sup> exactly
<div n="lb"/>the same list as the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā. The Maitrāyaṇī
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitā<sup>5</sup> has the list {%ayuta, prayuta,%} then {%ayuta%} again, {%arbuda,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%nyarbuda, samudra, madhya, anta, parārdha.%} The Pañcaviṃśa
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa<sup>6</sup> has the Vājasaneyi list up to {%nyarbuda%} inclusive,
<div n="lb"/>then follow {%nikharvaka, badva, akṣita,%} and apparently {%go%} =
<div n="lb"/>1,000,000,000,000. The Jaiminīya Upaniṣad Brāhmaṇa<sup>7</sup> list
[Page1-343+ 58]
<div n="lb"/>replaces {%nikharvaka%} by {%nikharva, badva%} by {%padma,%} and ends
<div n="lb"/>with {%akṣitir vyomāntaḥ.%} The Śāṅkhāyana Śrauta Sūtra<sup>8</sup> con-
<div n="lb"/>tinues the series after {%nyarbuda%} with {%nikharvāda, samudra,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%salila, antya, ananta%} (= 10 billions).
<div n="P"/>But beyond {%ayuta%}<sup>9</sup> none of these numbers has any vitality.
<div n="lb"/>{%Badva,%} indeed, occurs in the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa,<sup>10</sup> but it
<div n="lb"/>cannot there have any precise numerical sense;<sup>11</sup> and later on
<div n="lb"/>the names of these high numerals are very much confused.
<div n="P"/>An arithmetical progression of some interest is found in the
<div n="lb"/>Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa,<sup>12</sup> where occurs a list of sacrificial gifts
<div n="lb"/>in which each successive figure doubles the amount of the
<div n="lb"/>preceding one. It begins with {%dvādaśa-mānaṃ hiraṇyam,%} ‘gold
<div n="lb"/>to the value of 12’ (the unit being uncertain, but probably the
<div n="lb"/>{@Kṛṣṇala@}),<sup>13</sup> followed by ‘to the value of 24, 48, 96, 192, 384,
<div n="lb"/>768, 1,536, 3072,’ then {%dve aṣṭāviṃśati-śata-māne,%} which must
<div n="lb"/>mean 2×128×24 (the last unit being not a single {%māna,%} but
<div n="lb"/>a number of 24 {%mānas%}) = 6,144, then 12,288, 24,576, 49,152,
<div n="lb"/>98,304, 196,608, 393, 216. With these large numbers may be
<div n="lb"/>compared the minute theoretical subdivision of time found
<div n="lb"/>in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa,<sup>14</sup> where a day is divided into
<div n="lb"/>15 {%muhūrtas%} — 1 {%muhūrta%} = 15 {%kṣipras,%} 1 {%kṣipra%} = 15 {%etarhis,%}
<div n="lb"/>1 {%etarhi%} = 15 {%idānis,%} 1 {%idāni%} = 15 {%prāṇas.%} The Śāṅkhāyana Śrauta
<div n="lb"/>Sūtra<sup>15</sup> has a decimal division of the day into 15 {%muhūrtas%} — 
<div n="lb"/>1 {%muhūrta%} = 10 {%nimeṣas,%} 1 {%nimeṣa%} = 10 {%dhvaṃsis.%}
<div n="P"/>Few fractions are mentioned in Vedic literature. {%Ardha,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%pāda, śapha,%} and {%kalā%} denote (1/2), (1/4), (1/8), (1/16) respectively, but only
[Page1-344+ 61]
<div n="lb"/>the first two are common. {%Tṛtīya%} denotes the third part.<sup>16</sup>
<div n="lb"/>In the Rigveda<sup>17</sup> Indra and Viṣṇu are said to have divided
<div n="lb"/>1,000 by 3, though how they did so is uncertain. {%Tri-pād%}
<div n="lb"/>denotes ‘three-fourths.’<sup>18</sup>
<div n="P"/>There is no clear evidence that the Indians of the Vedic
<div n="lb"/>period had any knowledge of numerical figures, though it is
<div n="lb"/>perfectly possible.<sup>19</sup>
