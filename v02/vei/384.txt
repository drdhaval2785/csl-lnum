{@Ubhayā-dant,@}¦ ‘having incisors in both jaws,’ is an expression
<div n="lb"/>employed to distinguish, among domestic animals, the horse,
[Page1-098+ 47]
<div n="lb"/>the ass, etc., from the goat, the sheep, and cattle. The
<div n="lb"/>distinction occurs in a late hymn of the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> and is several
<div n="lb"/>times alluded to in the later Saṃhitās<sup>2</sup> and the Brāhmaṇas.<sup>3</sup>
<div n="lb"/>In one passage of the Taittirīya Saṃhitā<sup>4</sup> man is classed with
<div n="lb"/>the horse as {%ubhayā-dant.%} The opposite is {%anyato-dant,%} ‘having
<div n="lb"/>incisors in one jaw only,’ a term regularly applied to cattle,<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the eight incisors of which are, in fact, limited to the lower jaw.
<div n="lb"/>The ass is styled {%ubhayā-dant%} in the Atharvaveda.<sup>6</sup> In one
<div n="lb"/>passage of the Atharvaveda,<sup>7</sup> however, the epithet is applied to
<div n="lb"/>a ram; but the sense here is that a marvel occurs, just as in
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda<sup>8</sup> a ram destroys a lioness. Bloomfield<sup>9</sup> suggests
<div n="lb"/>in the Atharvaveda passage another reading which would mean
<div n="lb"/>‘horse.’ A parallel division of animals is that of the Taittirīya<sup>10</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and Vājasaneyi Saṃhitās<sup>11</sup> into ‘whole-hoofed’ ({%eka-śapha%}) and
<div n="lb"/>‘small’ ({%kṣudra%}).
<div n="P"/>Zimmer<sup>12</sup> seeks to show from the Greek <lang n="greek">ἀμφώδοντα</lang><sup>13</sup> and the
<div n="lb"/>Latin {%ambidens%}<sup>14</sup> that the Indo-European was familiar with
<div n="lb"/>the division of the five sacrificial animals into the two classes
<div n="lb"/>of man and horse on the one hand, and cattle, sheep, and
<div n="lb"/>goats on the other. But this supposition is not necessary.
