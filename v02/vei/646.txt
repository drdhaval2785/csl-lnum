{@Kuru-kṣetra@}¦ (‘land of the Kurus’) is always regarded in the
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa texts<sup>1</sup> as a particularly sacred country. Within its
<div n="lb"/>boundaries flowed the rivers {@Dṛṣadvatī@} and {@Sarasvatī,@} as well
<div n="lb"/>as the {@Āpayā.@}<sup>2</sup> Here, too, was situated {@Śaryaṇāvant,@}<sup>3</sup> which
[Page1-170+ 46]
<div n="lb"/>appears to have been a lake, like that known to the Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa by the name of {@Anyataḥ-plakṣā.@}<sup>4</sup> According to
<div n="lb"/>Pischel, there was also in Kurukṣetra a stream called {@Pastyā,@}<sup>5</sup>
<div n="lb"/>which he sees in certain passages of the Rigveda. The boun-
<div n="lb"/>daries of Kurukṣetra are given in a passage of the Taittirīya
<div n="lb"/>Āraṇyaka<sup>6</sup> as being Khāṇḍava on the south, the Tūrghna on
<div n="lb"/>the north, and the Parīṇah on the west. Roughly speaking, it
<div n="lb"/>corresponded to the modern Sirhind.
