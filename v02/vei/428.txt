{@Ṛtu,@}¦ ‘season,’ is a term repeatedly mentioned from the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>1</sup> onwards, Three seasons of the year are often
<div n="lb"/>alluded to,<sup>2</sup> but the names are not usually specified. In one
<div n="lb"/>passage of the Rigveda<sup>3</sup> spring ({%vasanta%}), summer ({%grīṣma%}), and
<div n="lb"/>autumn ({%śarad%}) are given. The Rigveda knows also the rainy
<div n="lb"/>season ({%prā-vṛṣ%}) and the winter ({%himā, hemanta%}). A more
<div n="lb"/>usual<sup>4</sup> division (not found in the Rigveda) is into five seasons,
[Page1-111+ 57]
<div n="lb"/>{%vasanta, grīṣma, varṣā, śarad, hemama-siśira,%} but occasionally
<div n="lb"/>the five are otherwise divided, {%varṣā-śarad%} being made one
<div n="lb"/>season.<sup>5</sup> Sometimes six<sup>6</sup> seasons are reckoned, {%hemanta%} and
<div n="lb"/>{%śiśira%} being divided, so that the six seasons can be made
<div n="lb"/>parallel to the twelve months of the year. A still more
<div n="lb"/>artificial arrangement<sup>7</sup> makes the seasons seven, possibly by
<div n="lb"/>reckoning the intercalary month as a season, as Weber and
<div n="lb"/>Zimmer<sup>8</sup> hold, or more probably because of the predilection
<div n="lb"/>for the number seven, as Roth<sup>9</sup> suggests. Occasionally the
<div n="lb"/>word {%ṛtu%} is applied to the months.<sup>10</sup> The last season,
<div n="lb"/>according to the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa,<sup>11</sup> is {%hemanta.%}
<div n="P"/>The growth of the division of the seasons from three to five
<div n="lb"/>is rightly explained by Zimmer<sup>12</sup> as indicating the advance of
<div n="lb"/>the Vedic Indians towards the east. It is not Rigvedic, but
<div n="lb"/>dominates the later Saṃhitās. Traces of an earlier division of
<div n="lb"/>the year into winter and summer do not appear clearly in the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda, where the appropriate words {%himā%} and {%samā%} are
<div n="lb"/>merely general appellations of the year, and where {%śarad%}<sup>13</sup> is
<div n="lb"/>commoner than either as a designation of the year, because it
<div n="lb"/>denotes the harvest, a time of overwhelming importance to a
<div n="lb"/>young agricultural people. The division of the year in one
<div n="lb"/>passage of the Atharvaveda<sup>14</sup> into two periods of six months
<div n="lb"/>is merely formal, and in no way an indication of old
<div n="lb"/>tradition.
