<L>1009<pc>1-273<k1>janamejaya<k2>janamejaya<h>1
1. {@Janam-ejaya@}¦ (‘man-impelling’) is the name of a king,
<div n="lb"/>a {@Pārikṣita,@}<sup>1</sup> famous towards the end of the Brāhmaṇa period.
<div n="lb"/>He is mentioned in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>2</sup> as owning
<div n="lb"/>horses which when wearied were refreshed with sweet drinks,
<div n="lb"/>and as a performer of the Aśvamedha, or horse sacrifice.<sup>3</sup> His
<div n="lb"/>capital, according to a Gāthā quoted in the Śatapatha<sup>4</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>the Aitareya Brāhmaṇas,<sup>5</sup> was {@Āsandīvant.@} His brothers
<div n="lb"/>{@Ugrasena, Bhīmasena,@} and {@Śrutasena@} are mentioned as having
[Page1-274+ 49]
<div n="lb"/>by the horse sacrifice purified themselves from sin. The priest
<div n="lb"/>who performed the sacrifice for him was {@Indrota Daivāpi@}
<div n="lb"/>{@Śaunaka.@}<sup>6</sup> On the other hand the Aitareya Brāhmaṇa,<sup>7</sup> which
<div n="lb"/>also mentions his Aśvamedha, names {@Tura Kāvaṣeya@} as his
<div n="lb"/>priest. It also contains an obscure tale stating that at one
<div n="lb"/>sacrifice of his he did not employ the {@Kaśyapas,@} but the
<div n="lb"/>{@Bhūtavīras,@} being, however, induced by the {@Asitamṛgas@} to
<div n="lb"/>have recourse to the Kaśyapas again.<sup>8</sup> He was a {@Kuru@} prince;
<div n="lb"/>see {@Parikṣit.@} The Gopatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>9</sup> tells an absurd tale
<div n="lb"/>about him, evidently as of an ancient hero.
