<L>265<pc>1-064<k1>Arya<k2>Arya
{@Ārya@}¦ is the normal designation in the Vedic literature from
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> onwards of an Āryan, a member of the three
<div n="lb"/>upper classes, {@Brāhmaṇa, Kṣatriya,@} or {@Vaiśya,@} as the formal
<div n="lb"/>division is given in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa.<sup>2</sup> The Ārya
<div n="lb"/>stands in opposition to the {@Dāsa,@}<sup>3</sup> but also to the {@Śūdra.@}
<div n="lb"/>Sometimes<sup>4</sup> the expression is restricted to the Vaiśya caste,
[Page1-065+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>the Brāhmaṇa and the Kṣatriya receiving special designations;
<div n="lb"/>but this use is not common, and it is often uncertain also
<div n="lb"/>whether {@Arya@} is not meant. The phrase Śūdrāryau<sup>5</sup> is espe-
<div n="lb"/>cially ambiguous, but appears to have denoted originally the
<div n="lb"/>Śūdra and the Āryan, for in the Mahāvrata ceremony the fight
<div n="lb"/>between a Śūdra and an Ārya is represented in the Taittirīya
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa as one between a Brāhmaṇa and a Śūdra, though
<div n="lb"/>the Sūtra treats it as a fight between a Vaiśya and a Śūdra.
<div n="P"/>The word Ārya (fem. Āryā or Ārī) also occurs frequently
<div n="lb"/>used as an adjective to describe the Āryan classes ({%viśaḥ%}),<sup>6</sup> or
<div n="lb"/>name ({%nāman%}),<sup>7</sup> or caste ({%varṇa%}),<sup>8</sup> or dwellings ({%dhāman%});<sup>9</sup> or
<div n="lb"/>again reference is made to the Āryan supremacy ({%vrata%})<sup>10</sup> being
<div n="lb"/>extended over the land. Āryan foes ({%vṛtra%})<sup>11</sup> are referred to
<div n="lb"/>beside Dāsa foes, and there are many<sup>12</sup> references to war
<div n="lb"/>of Āryan versus Āryan, as well as to war of Āryan against
<div n="lb"/>Dāsa. From this it can be fairly deduced that even by the
<div n="lb"/>time of the Rigveda the Āryan communities had advanced
<div n="lb"/>far beyond the stage of simple conquest of the aborigines. In
<div n="lb"/>the later Saṃhitās and Brāhmaṇas the wars alluded to seem
<div n="lb"/>mainly Āryan wars, no doubt in consequence of the fusion of
<div n="lb"/>Ārya and Dāsa into one community.
<div n="P"/>Weber<sup>13</sup> considers that the five peoples known to the Rigveda
<div n="lb"/>were the Āryans and the four peoples of the quarters ({%diś%})
<div n="lb"/>of the earth, but this is doubtful. Āryan speech ({%vāc%})<sup>14</sup>
<div n="lb"/>is specially referred to in the Aitareya and Śāṅkhāyana
<div n="lb"/>Āraṇyakas.
