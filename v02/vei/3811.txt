{@Hiraṇya@}¦ in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and later<sup>2</sup> denotes ‘gold.’ It is
<div n="lb"/>hardly possible to exaggerate the value attached to gold by the
<div n="lb"/>Vedic Indians. The metal was, it is clear, won from the bed of
<div n="lb"/>rivers. Hence the Indus is called ‘golden’<sup>3</sup> and ‘of golden
<div n="lb"/>stream.’<sup>4</sup> Apparently the extraction of gold from the earth was
<div n="lb"/>known,<sup>5</sup> and washing for gold is also recorded.<sup>6</sup>
<div n="P"/>Gold is the object of the wishes of the Vedic singer,<sup>7</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>golden treasures ({%hiraṇyāni%}) are mentioned as given by patrons<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>along with cows and horses. Gold was used for ornaments for
<div n="lb"/>neck and breast ({@Niṣka@}), for ear-rings ({@Karṇa-śobhana@}), and
<div n="lb"/>even for cups.<sup>9</sup> Gold is always associated with the gods.<sup>10</sup>
[Page2-505+ 62]
<div n="P"/>In the plural Hiraṇya denotes ‘ornaments of gold.’<sup>11</sup>
<div n="P"/>A gold currency was evidently beginning to be known in so
<div n="lb"/>far as definite weights of gold are mentioned: thus a weight,
<div n="lb"/>{%aṣṭā-prūḍ,%} occurs in the Saṃhitās,<sup>12</sup> and the golden {%śatamāna,%}
<div n="lb"/>‘weight of a hundred ({@Kṛṣṇalas@})’ is found in the same texts.<sup>13</sup>
<div n="lb"/>In several passages,<sup>14</sup> moreover, {%hiraṇya%} or {%hiraṇyāni%} may mean
<div n="lb"/>‘pieces of gold.’
<div n="P"/>Gold is described sometimes as {%harita,%}<sup>15</sup> ‘yellowish,’ some-
<div n="lb"/>times as {%rajata,%}<sup>16</sup> ‘whitish,’ when probably. ‘silver’ is alluded
<div n="lb"/>to. It was obtained from the ore by smelting.<sup>17</sup> Mega-
<div n="lb"/>sthenes<sup>18</sup> bears testimony to the richness in gold of India in
<div n="lb"/>his time.
