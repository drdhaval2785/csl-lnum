<L>969<pc>1-259<k1>cAturmAsya<k2>cAturmAsya
{@Cātur-māsya,@}¦ ‘four-monthly,’ denotes the festival of the
<div n="lb"/>Vedic ritual held at the beginning of the three seasons of four
<div n="lb"/>months each, into which the Vedic year was artificially divided.<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>It is clear that the sacrifices commenced with the beginning
<div n="lb"/>of each season,<sup>2</sup> and it is certain that the first of them, the
<div n="lb"/>Vaiśvadeva, coincided with the Phālgunī full moon,<sup>3</sup> the second,
<div n="lb"/>the Varuṇa-praghāsas, with the Āṣāḍhī full moon,<sup>4</sup> and the
<div n="lb"/>third, the Sāka-medha, with the Kārttikī full moon.<sup>5</sup> There
<div n="lb"/>were, however, two alternative datings: the festivals could also
<div n="lb"/>be held in the Caitrī, the Śrāvaṇī, and Āgrahāyaṇī (Mārgaśīrṣī)
<div n="lb"/>full moons,<sup>6</sup> or in the Vaiśākhī, Bhādrapadī, and Pauṣī full
<div n="lb"/>moons.<sup>7</sup> Neither of the later datings is found in a Brāhmaṇa
<div n="lb"/>text, but each may well have been known early, since the
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Saṃhitā<sup>8</sup> and the Pañcaviṃśa Brāhmaṇa<sup>9</sup> both
<div n="lb"/>recognize the full moon in the month Caitra as an alternative
<div n="lb"/>to the full moon in the month Phālguna, for the beginning of
<div n="lb"/>the year.
<div n="P"/>Jacobi considers that the commencement of the year with the
<div n="lb"/>full moon in the asterism Phalgunī, which is supported by other
<div n="lb"/>evidence,<sup>10</sup> indicates that the year at one time began with the winter
<div n="lb"/>solstice with the moon in Phalgunī, corresponding to the summer
<div n="lb"/>solstice when the sun was in Phalgunī. These astronomical
<div n="lb"/>conditions, he believes, existed in the time of the Rigveda,<sup>11</sup>
<div n="lb"/>and prevailed in the fourth millennium B.C. The alternative
[Page1-260+ 51]
<div n="lb"/>dates would then indicate periods when the winter solstice
<div n="lb"/>coincided with the Caitrī or the Vaiśākhī full moon. But
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg<sup>12</sup> and Thibaut<sup>13</sup> seem clearly right in holding that
<div n="lb"/>the coincidence of Phālgunī with the beginning of spring,<sup>14</sup>
<div n="lb"/>which is certain, is fatal to this view, and that there is no
<div n="lb"/>difficulty in regarding this date as consistent with the date of
<div n="lb"/>the winter solstice in the new moon of Māgha, which is given
<div n="lb"/>by the Kauṣītaki Brāhmaṇa,<sup>15</sup> and which forms the basis of
<div n="lb"/>the calculations of the Jyotiṣa.<sup>16</sup> The full moon in Phālguna
<div n="lb"/>would be placed about one month and a half after the winter
<div n="lb"/>solstice, or, say, in the first week of February, which date,
<div n="lb"/>according to Thibaut, may reasonably be deemed to mark the
<div n="lb"/>beginning of a new season in India about 800 B.C. At the
<div n="lb"/>same time it must be remembered that the date was necessarily
<div n="lb"/>artificial, inasmuch as the year was divided into three seasons,
<div n="lb"/>each of four months, and the Indian year does not in fact
<div n="lb"/>consist of three equal seasons. The variations of the other
<div n="lb"/>datings would then not be unnatural if any school wished to
<div n="lb"/>defer its spring festival, the Vaiśvadeva, to the time when
<div n="lb"/>spring had really manifested itself. See also {@Saṃvatsara.@}
