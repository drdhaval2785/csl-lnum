{@Otu@}¦ in Vedic literature<sup>1</sup> denotes the ‘woof’ in weaving and
<div n="lb"/>corresponds to {@Tantu,@} ‘the warp,’ the roots {%vā,%}<sup>2</sup> ‘to weave,’
<div n="lb"/>and {%tan,%}<sup>3</sup> to stretch,’ from which these two terms are derived,
<div n="lb"/>being used in parallel senses. In the process of weaving a
<div n="lb"/>shuttle ({@Tasara@}) was used. The ‘weaver’ is termed {%vāya,%}<sup>4</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>the ‘loom’ {%veman.%}<sup>5</sup> A wooden peg ({@Mayūkha@}) was used to
[Page1-124+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>stretch the web on, while lead was employed as a weight to
<div n="lb"/>extend it.<sup>6</sup>
<div n="P"/>The work of weaving was probably the special care of
<div n="lb"/>women.<sup>7</sup> A metaphor in the Atharvaveda<sup>8</sup> personifies Night
<div n="lb"/>and Day as two sisters weaving the web of the year, the
<div n="lb"/>nights serving as warp, the days as woof.
