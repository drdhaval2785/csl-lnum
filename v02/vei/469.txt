{@Oṣadhi.@}¦ — Roughly speaking, the vegetable world is divided
<div n="lb"/>in Vedic literature<sup>1</sup> between Oṣadhi or {@Vīrudh@} ‘plants’ and
<div n="lb"/>{@Vana@} or {@Vṛkṣa@} ‘trees.’ Oṣadhi is employed in opposition to
<div n="lb"/>Vīrudh to denote plants as possessing a healing power or some
<div n="lb"/>other quality useful to men, while Vīrudh is rather a generic
<div n="lb"/>term for minor vegetable growths, but sometimes,<sup>2</sup> when occur-
<div n="lb"/>ring beside Oṣadhi, signifies those plants which do not possess
<div n="lb"/>medicinal properties.
<div n="P"/>A list of the minor parts of which a plant is made up is given
<div n="lb"/>in the later Saṃhitās.<sup>3</sup> It comprises the root ({%mūla%}), the
<div n="lb"/>panicle ({%tūla%}), the stem ({%kāṇḍa%}), the twig ({%valśa%}), the flower
<div n="lb"/>({%puṣpa%}), and the fruit ({%phala%}), while trees<sup>4</sup> have, in addition, a
<div n="lb"/>corona ({%skandha%}), branches ({%śākhā%}), and leaves ({%parṇa%}). The
<div n="lb"/>Atharvaveda<sup>5</sup> gives an elaborate, though not very intelligible,
<div n="lb"/>division of plants into those which expand ({%pra-stṛṇatīḥ%}), are
<div n="lb"/>bushy ({%stambinīḥ%}), have only one sheath ({%eka-śuṅgāḥ%}), are
<div n="lb"/>creepers ({%pra-tanvatīḥ%}), have many stalks ({%aṃśumatīḥ%}), are
[Page1-126+ 35]
<div n="lb"/>jointed ({%kāṇḍinīḥ%}), or have spreading branches ({%vi-śākhāḥ%}). In
<div n="lb"/>the Rigveda<sup>6</sup> plants are termed ‘fruitful’ ({%phalinīḥ%}), ‘blossom-
<div n="lb"/>ing’ ({%puṣpavatīḥ%}), and ‘having flowers’ ({%pra-sūvarīḥ%}).
