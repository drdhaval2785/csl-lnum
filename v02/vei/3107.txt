{@Vyādhi,@}¦ ‘disease,’ occurs several times in Vedic literature.<sup>1</sup>
<div n="lb"/>The specific diseases are dealt with under the separate names,
<div n="lb"/>but the Vedic texts also mention innumerable bodily defects.
<div n="lb"/>The list of victims<sup>2</sup> at the Puruṣamedha (‘human sacrifice’)
<div n="lb"/>includes a ‘dwarf’ ({%vāmana, kubja%}), a ‘bald’ person ({%khalati%}),<sup>3</sup> a
<div n="lb"/>‘blind’ man ({%andha%}),<sup>4</sup> a ‘deaf’ man ({%badhira%}),<sup>5</sup> a ‘dumb’ man
<div n="lb"/>({%mūka%}),<sup>6</sup> a ‘fat’ man ({%pīvan%}), a ‘leper’ ({%sidhmala, kilāsa%}),<sup>7</sup> a
<div n="lb"/>‘yellow-eyed’ man ({%hary-akṣa%}), a ‘tawny-eyed’ man ({%piṅg-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%ākṣa%}), a ‘cripple’ ({%pīṭha-sarpin%}), a ‘lame’ man ({%srāma%}), a
<div n="lb"/>‘sleepless’ man ({%jāgaraṇa%}), a ‘sleepy’ man ({%svapana%}), one<sup>8</sup> ‘too
<div n="lb"/>tall’ ({%ati-dīrgha%}), one ‘too short’ ({%ati-hrasva%}), one ‘too stout’
<div n="lb"/>({%ati-sthāla%} or {%aty-aṃsala%}), one ‘too thin’ ({%ati-kṛśa%}), one ‘too
<div n="lb"/>white’ ({%ati-śukla%}), one ‘too dark’ ({%ati-kṛṣṇa%}), one ‘too bald’
<div n="lb"/>({%ati-kulva%}), and one ‘too hairy’ ({%ati-lomaśa%}).
<div n="P"/>In the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>9</sup> the man with bad nails and the
<div n="lb"/>man with brown teeth are mentioned along with sinners like
<div n="lb"/>the {@Didhiṣūpati.@} The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>10</sup> mentions a
<div n="lb"/>‘white-spotted ({%śukla%}), bald-headed man, with projecting teeth
<div n="lb"/>({%viklidha%}) and reddish-brown eyes.’ Interesting is Zimmer's<sup>11</sup>
<div n="lb"/>suggestion that {%kirmira%} found in the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>12</sup>
<div n="lb"/>means ‘spotty’ as an intermixture of races, but it is only a
<div n="lb"/>conjecture, apparently based on a supposed connexion of the
<div n="lb"/>word with {%kṛ,%} ‘mix.’ In the Vājasaneyi Saṃhitā<sup>13</sup> and the
[Page2-339+ 57]
<div n="lb"/>Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>14</sup> various epithets are applied to women,
<div n="lb"/>some of which seem to denote disease, and in the Atharvaveda<sup>15</sup>
<div n="lb"/>the feminine adjectives, ‘antelope-footed’ ({%ṛśya-padī%}) and ‘bull-
<div n="lb"/>toothed’ ({%vṛṣa-datī%}), probably refer to bodily defects.
