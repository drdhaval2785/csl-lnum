{@Prāṇa@}¦ is also used in a wider sense to denote the organs of
<div n="lb"/>sense,<sup>13</sup> or as Sāyaṇa<sup>14</sup> puts it, the ‘orifices of the head,’ etc.
<div n="lb"/>These are given as six in one passage of the Śatapatha
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa,<sup>15</sup> presumably the eyes, ears, and nostrils. More
<div n="lb"/>frequently there are stated to be seven in the head, the mouth
<div n="lb"/>being then included.<sup>16</sup> Sometimes again they are mentioned
[Page2-048+ 65]
<div n="lb"/>as nine,<sup>17</sup> or as seven in the head and two below.<sup>18</sup> Ten are
<div n="lb"/>counted in the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa<sup>19</sup> and the Jaiminīya
<div n="lb"/>Brāhmaṇa,<sup>20</sup> while even eleven are mentioned in the Kāṭhaka
<div n="lb"/>Upaniṣad,<sup>21</sup> and twelve in the Kāṭhaka Saṃhitā,<sup>22</sup> where the
<div n="lb"/>two breasts are added. Exactly what organs are taken to
<div n="lb"/>make up the numbers beyond seven is not certain.<sup>23</sup> The tenth
<div n="lb"/>is the navel ({%nābhi%}) in the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā;<sup>24</sup> when eleven
<div n="lb"/>are named the Brahma-randhra<sup>25</sup> (suture in the crown) may be
<div n="lb"/>included; in the Atharvaveda,<sup>26</sup> as interpreted by the Bṛhad-
<div n="lb"/>āraṇyaka Upaniṣad,<sup>27</sup> the seventh and eighth are the organs
<div n="lb"/>of taste and speech respectively. But usually these make one
<div n="lb"/>only, and the eighth and ninth are either in the breast<sup>28</sup> or
<div n="lb"/>below (the organs of evacuation).<sup>29</sup>
<div n="P"/>The word Prāṇa has sometimes merely the general sense of
<div n="lb"/>breath, even when opposed to Apāna.<sup>30</sup> But its proper sense
<div n="lb"/>is beyond question ‘breathing forth,’ ‘expiration,’ and not as
<div n="lb"/>the St. Petersburg Dictionary explains it, ‘the breath inspired,’
<div n="lb"/>a version due to the desire to interpret Apāna as ‘expiration,’
<div n="lb"/>a meaning suggested by the preposition {%apa,%} ‘away.’ This
<div n="lb"/>being clearly shown both by the native scholiasts<sup>31</sup> and by
<div n="lb"/>other evidence,<sup>32</sup> Böhtlingk<sup>33</sup> later accepted the new view.
