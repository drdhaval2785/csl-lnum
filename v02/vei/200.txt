{@Ahan,@}¦ ‘day.’ Like other peoples, the Indians used night
<div n="lb"/>as a general expression of time as well as day, but by no means
<div n="lb"/>predominantly.<sup>1</sup> Night is also termed the dark ({%kṛṣṇa%}), as
[Page1-049+ 56]
<div n="lb"/>opposed to the light ({%arjuna%}), day.<sup>2</sup> Aho-rātra<sup>3</sup> is a regular
<div n="lb"/>term for ‘day and night’ combined.
<div n="P"/>The day itself is variously divided. In the Atharvaveda<sup>4</sup>
<div n="lb"/>a division into ‘the rising sun’ ({%udyan sūryaḥ%}), ‘the coming
<div n="lb"/>together of the cows’ ({%saṃ-gava%}), ‘midday’ ({%madhyaṃ-dina%}),
<div n="lb"/>‘afternoon’ ({%aparāhṇa%}), and ‘sunset’ ({%astaṃ-yan%}) is found.
<div n="lb"/>In the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>5</sup> the same series appears with
<div n="lb"/>‘early’ ({%prātar%}) and ‘evening’ ({%sāyāhna%}) substituted for the
<div n="lb"/>first and last members, while a shorter list gives {%prātar,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%saṃgava, sāyam.%} In the Maitrāyaṇī Saṃhitā<sup>6</sup> there is the
<div n="lb"/>series ‘dawn’ {%(uṣas), saṃgava, madhyaṃdina,%} and {%aparāhṇa.%}
<div n="P"/>The morning is also, according to Zimmer, called {%api-śarvara,%}<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>as the time when the dark is just past. It is named {%svasara,%}<sup>8</sup>
<div n="lb"/>as the time when the cows are feeding, before the first milking
<div n="lb"/>at the {%saṃgava,%} or when the birds are awakening.<sup>9</sup> It is also
<div n="lb"/>called {%pra-pitva,%}<sup>10</sup> according to Zimmer.<sup>11</sup> But Geldner<sup>12</sup> points
<div n="lb"/>out that that term refers to the late midday, which also is
<div n="lb"/>called {%api-śarvara,%} as bordering on the coming night, being the
<div n="lb"/>time when day is hastening to its close, as in a race. From
<div n="lb"/>another point of view, evening is called {%abhi-pitva,%}<sup>13</sup> the time
<div n="lb"/>when all come to rest. Or again, morning and evening are
<div n="lb"/>denoted as the dawning of the sun ({%uditā sūryasya%}), or its
<div n="lb"/>setting ({%ni-mruc%}). The midday is regularly {%madhyam ahnām,%}<sup>14</sup>
<div n="lb"/>{%madhye,%}<sup>15</sup> or {%madhyaṃdina. Saṃgava%}<sup>16</sup> is the forenoon, between
<div n="lb"/>the early morning ({%prātar%}) and midday ({%madhyaṃdina%}).
<div n="P"/>The divisions of time less than the day are seldom precisely
<div n="lb"/>given. In the Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa,<sup>17</sup> however, a day and
[Page1-050+ 48]
<div n="lb"/>night make up 30 {%muhūrtas;%} 1 {%muhūrta%} = 15 {%kṣipra;%} 1 {%kṣipra%} =
<div n="lb"/>15 {%etarhi;%} 1 {%etarhi%} = 15 {%idāni;%} 1 {%idāni%} = 15 breathings; 1 breath-
<div n="lb"/>ing = 1 spiration; 1 spiration = 1 twinkling ({%nimeṣa%}), etc. In
<div n="lb"/>the Sāṅkhāyana Āraṇyaka<sup>18</sup> the series is {%dhvaṃsayo, nimeṣāḥ,%}
<div n="lb"/>{%kāṣṭhāḥ, kalāḥ, kṣanā, muhūrtā, ahorātrāḥ.%} A thirtyfold division
<div n="lb"/>of day as well as of night is seen in one passage of the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>19</sup> by Zimmer, who compares the Babylonian sixty-
<div n="lb"/>fold division of the day and night. But the expression used — 
<div n="lb"/>thirty Yojanas — is too vague and obscure — Bergaigne<sup>20</sup> refers
<div n="lb"/>it to the firmament — to build any theory upon with safety.
<div n="P"/>The longer divisions of time are regularly ‘half month’
<div n="lb"/>({%ardha-māsa%}), ‘month’ ({%māsa%}), ‘season’ ({%ṛtu%}), and ‘year’
<div n="lb"/>({%saṃvatsara%}), which often<sup>21</sup> occur in this sequence after
<div n="lb"/>{%ahorātrāṇi%} (‘days and nights’).
