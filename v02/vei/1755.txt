{@Pāka-dūrvā@}¦ is, in a verse of the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> included with
<div n="lb"/>{@Kiyāmbu@} and {@Vyalkaśā@} among the plants used for growing on
<div n="lb"/>the spot where the corpse of the dead man has been consumed
<div n="lb"/>with fire.<sup>2</sup> The verse is repeated in the Taittirīya Āraṇyaka<sup>3</sup>

<div n="lb"/>with the variant Kyāmbu. In the Atharvaveda<sup>4</sup> the word is
<div n="lb"/>read Śāṇḍadūrvā. Pākadūrvā is probably, as Sāyaṇa-under-
<div n="lb"/>stands it, {%paripakva-dūrvā,%} ‘ripe or edible millet.’ Śāṇḍadūrvā
<div n="lb"/>is explained by the commentator<sup>5</sup> in various ways, as millet
<div n="lb"/>‘having egg-shaped roots’ ({%i.e., sāṇḍa,%} not {%śāṇḍa%}), or as ‘having
<div n="lb"/>long joints,’ with the additional remark that it was called {%bṛhad-%}
<div n="lb"/>{%dūrvā,%} ‘large millet.’ In the Taittirīya Āraṇyaka, on the other
<div n="lb"/>hand, the commentary explains Pākadūrvā as small millet.
