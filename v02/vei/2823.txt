1. {@Varṇa,@}¦ ‘colour,’ is a common word in the Rigveda<sup>1</sup> and
<div n="lb"/>later.<sup>2</sup> A large number of colours are enumerated in Vedic
<div n="lb"/>literature, but it is not possible to deduce any clear information
<div n="lb"/>as to the accuracy with which the Vedic Indian distinguished
<div n="lb"/>colours, or as to the principle on which his distinctions were based.
<div n="lb"/>The Rigveda seems to show that red or yellow colours were
<div n="lb"/>the most noticed, but this may be accidental.<sup>3</sup> ‘Black’ or
<div n="lb"/>‘dark’ is denoted by {%kṛṣṇa,%} ‘white’ or ‘light-coloured’ by {%śukla%}
<div n="lb"/>or {%śveta.%} ‘Black’ seems to be meant in one passage of the
<div n="lb"/>Rigveda<sup>4</sup> by {%śyenī%} also. ‘Dark-grey’ or ‘dusky’ is expressed
<div n="lb"/>by {%śyāma.%}<sup>5</sup> The sense of {%nīla%}<sup>6</sup> is doubtful, perhaps ‘dark-blue,’
<div n="lb"/>‘bluish-black.’ The series of words {%hari, hariṇa, harit, harita,%}
<div n="lb"/>seems, on the whole, to denote ‘yellow,’ but ‘green’ is also a
<div n="lb"/>possible rendering, since the epithet is used of the frog.<sup>7</sup>
<div n="lb"/>‘Brown’ is certainly the meaning of {%babhru,%} which is used of
<div n="lb"/>the {@Vibhītaka@} nut (see {@Akṣa@}). ‘Reddish-brown’ seems to be
<div n="lb"/>the tinge implied by {%kapila%}<sup>8</sup> (‘monkey-coloured’), while {%piṅgala%}
<div n="lb"/>appears to denote a shade of brown in which yellow pre-
[Page2-247+ 66]
<div n="lb"/>dominates, ‘tawny.’<sup>9</sup> ‘Yellow’ is expressed by {%pīta%} as well as
<div n="lb"/>{%pāṇḍu.%}<sup>10</sup> A garment of saffron ({%māhārajana%}) is mentioned in the
<div n="lb"/>Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad.<sup>11</sup> {%Rudhira%} and {%lohita%} are red, while
<div n="lb"/>{%aruṇa%} is ‘ruddy.’ {%Kalmāṣa%} means ‘spotted,’<sup>12</sup> and {%śilpa%}
<div n="lb"/>‘dappled,’<sup>13</sup> while mingled shades like {%aruṇa-piśaṅga,%} ‘reddish
<div n="lb"/>brown,’ also occur.<sup>14</sup>
