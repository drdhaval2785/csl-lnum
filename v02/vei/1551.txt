{@Nārāśaṃsī@}¦ (scil. Ṛc), ‘(verse) celebrating men,’ is mentioned
<div n="lb"/>as early as the Rigveda,<sup>1</sup> and is distinguished from {@Gāthā@} in
<div n="lb"/>a number of passages in the later literature.<sup>2</sup> The Kāṭhaka
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitā,<sup>3</sup> while distinguishing the two, asserts that both are
<div n="lb"/>false ({%anṛtam%}). It is hardly probable that the two were abso-
<div n="lb"/>lutely distinct, for the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa<sup>4</sup> has the phrase
<div n="lb"/>‘a Gāthā celebrating men’ ({%nārāśaṃsī%}). What such verses
<div n="lb"/>were may be seen from the Śāṅkhāyana Śrauta Sūtra,<sup>5</sup> which
<div n="lb"/>enumerates the {%Nārāśaṃsāni%} at the Puruṣamedha, or ‘human
[Page1-446+ 63]
<div n="lb"/>sacrifice.’ They may legitimately be reckoned as a source of
<div n="lb"/>the epic.<sup>6</sup>
<div n="P"/>The term Nārāśaṃsī is restricted in some passages<sup>7</sup> to a
<div n="lb"/>particular group of three verses of the Atharvaveda,<sup>8</sup> but
<div n="lb"/>Oldenberg<sup>9</sup> must be right in holding that the restricted sense
<div n="lb"/>is not to be read into the Rigveda.<sup>10</sup> Not even in the Taittirīya
<div n="lb"/>Saṃhitā<sup>11</sup> is the technical sense certain, and the Bṛhaddevatā<sup>12</sup>
<div n="lb"/>gives the word a general application.
